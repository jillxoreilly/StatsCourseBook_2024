Tutor Notes#

This version of the notebook has the answers filled in for your convenience!

Here are some things to think about for your interactions with students:

The first set of exercises (“Heat”) cover basic indexing tasks. It is essential that students master this.

  • You may refer them back to the prepwork sheet on descriptives and indexing to work through it again if struggling.

The second set of exercises (“Rain”) are supposed to illustrate that different descriptive statistics can be used to answer different questions. The question “which is the rainiest month” has several answers, depending how you define “rainiest”.

  • First year students are fresh from school and are often used to a style of learning in which they are given very prescriptive instructions; the thing they need to learn is simmply the mechanics of carrying out a calculation. As university students, they need to understand how we work out the right question to ask of our data and how we formalize such questions in terms of descriptive statistics.

The third set of exercises (“Snow”) introduce the idea of creating a new variable fromm a combination of others.

  • How will we work out which days might have had snow?

  • Will we count a day as snowy even if a tiny amount fell, or should we set a higher threshold?

  • These exercises also provide further practice in indexing.

b. What is the proportion of wet days overall?#

Hint: The values True and False can be treated as 1 and 0 respectively.

To get the proportion of days on which wet==True, we can use a programmming trick which is to simply take the mean of the column wet:

  • say there are 100 days in my sample

    • say 66 of them, wet==True==1

    • for the other 44, wet==False==0

  • If we take the mean, this gives us the proportion of wet days because we:

    • add up all the values (answer=66)

    • divide by the number of cases (100)

    • result is 66/100 = 0.66 or 66%, the proportion of wet days

# your code here
weather.wet.mean()

0.46311481154423
